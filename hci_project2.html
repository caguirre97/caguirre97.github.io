COVID-19 in Minorities

How has COVID-19 affected racial/ethnic minorities?

COVID-19 has impacted all of us, but it is important to recognize that minority groups have been impacted disproportionately. For our analysis, we decided to look at data surrounding COVID-19 cases, hospitalizations, and deaths across different US localities and investigate deeper into how various ethnic groups have been affected by this pandemic. Specifically, our data is aggregated from The COVID Tracking Project, the Economic Policy Institute, and KFF. These datasets provide information about COVID cases, unemployment, and ethnic population distributions across all 50 US states. The goal of our project is to highlight the inequity that exists in the public health system and show how pandemics like this one can have significant impacts on underrepresented communities.

COVID-19 Cases in Minorities

Figure 1. COVID-19 Cases of racial/ethnic minorities by region.
The above chart depicts COVID case breakdowns for minority groups across the 4 Census regions in the United States; namely, the West, the Midwest, the Northeast, and the South. The larger pie chart shows that minority groups were significantly impacted in the West and South regions, with lots of minority cases stemming from Florida, Texas, and California. The larger pie chart is further broken down into smaller pie charts within each region that provide indication as to how many COVID cases were found for each minority group listed in the dataset. The COVID Tracking Project specifically listed cases into the following minority groups: Black, LatinX, Asian, American Indian and Alaska Native, Native Hawaiian and Pacific Islander, and Multiracial. The majority of cases, understandably, come from the larger population groups i.e. Black and LatinX. As such, we can look at normalized state-by-state breakdowns in the following chart.

Normalized Cases and Deaths by State

Click the race labels for either "Cases" or "Deaths" to toggle the Heat Map's data display

Figure 2. COVID-19 Cases and Deaths of racial/ethnic groups by state.

Nearly a year into the coronavirus pandemic and we are all well accustomed to seeing maps that show the largest states like California, New York, and Texas have more COVID-19 cases than any other states in the nation. However, these simple case-count rankings do not account for population densities and fail to show who is actually affected the most.

This series of graphs highlights which demographic populations have experienced the highest rates of COVID-19 cases and deaths proportional to how many individuals make up each demographic. These numbers are broken down on the state-by-state level as well as for the nation as a whole.

How To Read

The dual-axis chart on the left displays the number of cases (blue) and deaths (red) per 1,000 people per ethnic group -- White, Black, LatinX, Asian, American Indian/Alaska Native (AIAN), Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander (NHPI), or Multiracial. The default state displays the breakdown for the United States as a whole, and by clicking an individual state from the heat map you will see the graphs change to reflect the COVID-19 numbers broken down to that state’s specific population distribution.

The heat map on the right directly compares how each state measures up from the same data (cases/deaths per 1,000 people). The darker the state’s color, the more densely that population has been affected by COVID-19. By clicking on the race labels from the chart on the left, the heat map will change to reflect breakdown by that race. For example, clicking the “LatinX” label from the right side of the dual-axis graph will show which states have the highest rate of LatinX COVID-19 deaths.

Analysis

Normalizing COVID-19 cases and deaths to demographic population distributions allows us to see inequalities that may otherwise remain invisible. For example, in New York 3,000 more White people have died from COVID-19 than Black individuals, however this is extremely misleading. Once we account for the number of White and Black people living in New York, we see that the Black peope ate dying 3 times as frequently relative to Whites.

This trend is not specific to just New York. Black, LatinX, and Indiginous people have been affected the most by COVID-19 deaths across the nation and this is apparent just by clicking through the states and seeing that the White death line rarely passes above that of minority populations.

It is easy to read big numbers and brush them away, but by taking the time and responsibility to look with more scrutiny, we ensure to give under-represented minorities the attention they deserve.

Data

Unemployment

Figure 3. Cummulative COVID-19 Cases racial/ethnic groups by date.
Figure 4. Change in Unemployment Rate from Q1 to Q2 in 2020.
Now let’s look at a longitudinal view of COVID-19, specifically at how it has affected minority populations and how unemployment rates also affect these communities unequally.
Methodology.
Figure 3 shows the cumulative COVID-19 cases scaled by population from March to November 2020. The COVID-19 cases by racial/ethnic groups were collected from [3]. We convert the COVID-19 cases numbers to percent of infected population using the demographic information by state collected from [2]. This view enables us to observe and make a fair comparison of COVID-19 cases between demographic groups as the concentration of infected population. Figure 4 shows the unemployment percent change between Q1 and Q2 of 2020 by demographic group. We collected this information from [3].
Analysis.
In Figure 3, from the start, we observe that COVID-19 has affected minority groups, specifically Latinx and Black, disproportionally compared to the White population. Minority communities have been traditionally underserved and undertreated by the health department, and our data shows that the COVID-19 crisis is not an exception. The infection rate of Black and Latinx communities is extremely high compared to White individuals, and this difference became bigger as we moved into the end of the summer: Black and Latinx are infected twice or more times as we get to September. Since the fall, the latest infection information has started to level the infection rate among demographics as COVID-19 starts to spread in the rural, predominately white, population. Although the differences in infection rates between demographic groups have decreased, the Black and Latinx communities are still affected disproportionately. If we combine the information from Figure 3 and Figure 4, we observe that minority populations are unequally affected by both unemployment rate and infection rate. Unemployment rate has affected the Black and Latinx communities more (with unemployment rate change close to twice as it is for the White population). Intuitively this makes sense as more people get sick with the pandemic, they are more likely to lose their jobs, and unemployment rates spike for such communities. But, while this is an intuitive remark, it is still left to be seen how much the combination of unemployment and infection rate affects our minority communities in the US.
